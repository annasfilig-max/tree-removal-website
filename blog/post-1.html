Signs a Tree Needs to Come Down

April 15, 2026 · By [Author Name] · 7 min read

Most homeowners never think about the trees on their property until something goes wrong — a branch crashes through the roof during a storm, a trunk starts leaning toward the driveway, or a neighbor mentions the big oak looks "a little off." By the time the problem is obvious, it's often already serious. The good news is that trees almost always give you warning signs before they fail. Learning to recognize them can save you thousands in property damage and, more importantly, prevent someone from getting hurt.

Here are the signs worth paying attention to, and what each one typically means.

A Sudden or Worsening Lean

Some trees have grown at an angle their whole lives — that's not necessarily a problem. What matters is whether the lean is new or getting worse. If you can see fresh soil lifting on one side of the base, cracked earth at the opposite side, or exposed roots that weren't visible before, the tree's anchor system is giving way. A sudden lean after a storm is especially urgent. Trees in this condition can fail with no additional warning, particularly in wet soil or high wind.

Dead Limbs and Sparse Canopy

In the growing season, a healthy tree should be full of leaves across most of its canopy. When you see large branches that never leaf out, or limbs with bark peeling off and no buds, those branches are dead. A few dead limbs here and there may just need pruning, but when dead wood makes up a significant portion of the canopy — say, a quarter or more — the tree itself is in decline. Dead limbs also fall without warning, and large ones can weigh hundreds of pounds.

Root Problems

The roots are the part of the tree you can't see, which is exactly why root issues are so dangerous. Some of the clearest warning signs show up at the base of the trunk:

  • Mushrooms or fungal growth at the base — often a sign of internal decay
  • Exposed or heaving roots, especially on one side
  • Cracked or raised soil around the root flare
  • A hollow sound when you tap the lower trunk with a mallet
  • Recent construction, trenching, or soil grading within the drip line
  • Standing water or compacted soil around the roots

Any one of these is worth a closer look. Two or more together usually means the tree's structural integrity is compromised.

Cracks, Cavities, and Hollow Trunks

Vertical cracks that run through the bark and into the wood are a red flag, especially if they appear on a main stem or where large branches meet the trunk. Large cavities — the kind squirrels and raccoons live in — aren't automatically fatal, but they reduce the wood's strength and can hide extensive internal decay. A certified arborist can tap and probe these areas to estimate how much sound wood is left. When more than about one-third of the trunk's cross-section is compromised, removal is usually the safer option.

A leaning trunk, a cavity, and a dead limb on the same tree is not three small problems — it's one big one. When warning signs stack up, trust them.

Proximity Matters

Not every struggling tree needs to come down immediately. A dying tree in the back corner of a large wooded lot can often be left to nature. A dying tree leaning toward your house, garage, power line, or your neighbor's shed is a very different situation. Proximity to what the industry calls "targets" — anything of value that could be damaged if the tree falls — is what moves a tree from "monitor" to "remove." The higher the target value and the more severe the warning signs, the less room there is to wait.

When in Doubt, Get a Professional Assessment

Pictures on a website can only take you so far. If you're seeing one or more of the signs above, the smartest next step is to have a qualified professional walk the tree with you. A good arborist or tree service company will give you an honest read — sometimes the answer is removal, sometimes it's strategic pruning, and sometimes it's simply keeping an eye on things. The wrong move is ignoring it until the tree decides for you.
