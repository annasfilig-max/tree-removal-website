What to Look For When Hiring a Tree Service

April 1, 2026 · By [Author Name] · 6 min read

Tree work is one of the most dangerous jobs in the country, and one of the easiest industries for bad operators to hide in. The barrier to entry is low — a pickup truck, a chainsaw, and a magnetic sign — but the consequences of hiring the wrong crew can be huge: damage to your home, injuries on your property that fall back on you, or a half-finished job where nobody answers the phone. The good news is that separating a legitimate tree service from a fly-by-night operation is straightforward if you know what to ask for.

Insurance Comes First

Before anything else, ask for proof of two specific types of coverage: general liability insurance (which covers damage to your property) and workers' compensation (which covers injuries to the crew while they're working on your property). A reputable company will provide a certificate of insurance directly from their insurer — not a photo of a policy page, not a verbal assurance. If they hesitate, say they're "self-insured," or promise to "send it later," walk away.

Workers' comp is especially important. If an uninsured worker gets hurt climbing a tree on your property, you can end up personally liable for their medical bills. No price savings is worth that risk.

Credentials and Experience

In the tree care industry, the most widely respected credential is ISA Certified Arborist — a certification from the International Society of Arboriculture that requires passing an exam and maintaining continuing education. Not every crew member needs to be certified, but a reputable company should have at least one certified arborist on staff who oversees the work. State-specific contractor licenses are also common; ask for the license number and verify it with your state's licensing board if you want to be thorough.

Get It in Writing

Never — and this bears repeating — never pay for tree work based on a verbal estimate. A legitimate service will provide a written quote that specifies the scope of work, who's responsible for cleanup, the timeline, the price, and the payment terms. A written estimate protects both of you. It also forces the company to think through the job carefully before committing to a price, which is why the bargain-basement quotes from verbal-only operators so often balloon mid-job.

References and Reviews

In the age of smartphones, any company that's been around for more than a year or two has reviews online. Check Google, Facebook, and the Better Business Bureau. Look for patterns: does the company respond professionally to negative reviews? Are there consistent complaints about surprise charges or incomplete cleanup? A handful of bad reviews out of hundreds is normal and not a dealbreaker. A cluster of identical complaints is a warning.

Questions to Ask Before You Sign

Once you're close to hiring someone, have a short phone call or in-person chat and ask the following:

  • Who specifically will be doing the work — employees or subcontractors?
  • Does the quote include full cleanup, stump removal if needed, and hauling?
  • How do you handle surprises — like hidden decay discovered mid-job?
  • What's your warranty or guarantee on the work?
  • What's your payment schedule, and do you require a deposit?
  • If weather or equipment issues delay us, what does rescheduling look like?

Any reputable crew will answer these easily. If you get vague, rushed, or defensive answers, keep looking.

The cheapest quote is almost never the best deal. Price is what you pay. Value is what you get. With tree work, the gap between the two can be enormous.

Red Flags to Walk Away From

Be cautious of any company that shows up unsolicited at your door after a storm, offers a deep discount only if you commit today, has no physical address or verifiable business listing, demands full payment up front before any work begins, uses a magnetic sign on an unmarked truck, or can't produce current insurance certificates. These aren't universal deal-breakers — there are legitimate small operators — but stack two or three of them and you're looking at a problem.

Comparing Bids Fairly

When you have two or three written estimates in hand, make sure you're comparing equivalent scopes of work. One quote that's "$800 cheaper" might not include stump grinding, or might leave all the debris piled in your yard, or might only remove the tree to the lower trunk. Ask each company to confirm in writing exactly what isn't included — that list is often more useful than the list of what is. When the scopes match, you can compare prices honestly and make a real decision.

Hiring the right tree service isn't complicated. It's just about doing a little homework before you sign. A little homework up front saves a lot of headaches later, and gets you the crew you'd actually want working near your house.
