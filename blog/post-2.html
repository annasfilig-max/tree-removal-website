How to Care for the Trees on Your Property Year-Round

April 8, 2026 · By [Author Name] · 8 min read

Trees aren't low-maintenance — they just seem that way because their maintenance cycles are long. A tree that's been ignored for a decade can go from healthy to hazardous without looking dramatically different along the way. The homeowners who get the most out of their trees (and the fewest emergencies) are the ones who give them a little attention at each stage of the year. Here's what that looks like, season by season.

Spring: Inspect and Recover

Once the leaves start coming in, you can see what winter did to your trees. Walk each major tree slowly and look up. Are there broken branches hanging in the canopy? Is the canopy symmetric, or are there bare patches where you'd expect leaves by now? Did the bark split anywhere, especially on the south or southwest side where winter sun caused rapid temperature swings? Mark anything that looks concerning and plan to address it before early summer.

Spring is also the time to refresh mulch rings around young and mid-age trees. A two-to-four-inch layer of mulch, kept a few inches away from the trunk itself, helps the soil retain moisture and protects surface roots from mower damage. Avoid the so-called "mulch volcano" — piling mulch against the trunk traps moisture and encourages rot where you don't want it. Young trees also benefit from a light, slow-release fertilizer if the soil is poor.

Summer: Water Deep, Prune Lightly

Most of the tree stress we see in mid-summer comes down to one thing: shallow, frequent watering that never reaches the root zone. A long slow soak once a week is worth far more than a daily sprinkle. Newly planted trees especially need deep watering for their first two growing seasons — roughly ten to fifteen gallons a week during dry stretches.

Summer is generally a bad time for heavy pruning. Trees are actively growing, and large cuts can stress them and attract pests. Limit summer work to removing clearly dead wood, small crossing branches, and anything that's interfering with structures or walkways. Watch for early signs of pest or disease trouble: yellowing leaves out of season, sticky residue under the canopy, unusual spots on foliage, or wilting in mid-morning despite adequate water.

Fall: Clean Up and Prepare

As leaves drop, it gets easier to see the structure of each tree. This is a great moment to walk your property with fresh eyes and identify anything that needs attention before winter. Key tasks:

  • Rake and compost leaves so thick layers don't smother turf or promote fungal problems
  • Clear gutters and pay attention to any branches hanging over your roof
  • Walk the drip line of each major tree and note any new root heaving or soil cracks
  • Deep-water trees one last time before the ground freezes if fall has been dry
  • Schedule any late-season pruning that's better done while the tree is going dormant
  • Identify limbs over driveways, parking areas, or the house that could fail under snow or ice
The best time to trim a hazardous limb is before you know it's hazardous. Fall is when most preventable winter storm damage gets prevented.

Winter: Prune, Protect, and Plan

Winter — once trees are fully dormant — is the ideal time for major structural pruning on most hardwood species. The tree isn't putting energy into growth, sap flow is low, pest activity is minimal, and without leaves your crew can actually see the branch structure clearly. Proper winter pruning shapes a tree for the next twenty years of life. Improper pruning, whether by an untrained crew or with the wrong tools, can do damage that shows up years later.

Winter is also the season to protect young trunks from rodents chewing at the bark under the snow line, and from sun scald on warm late-winter afternoons. A simple tree wrap or a length of white plastic guard solves both problems cheaply. If ice storms are common in your area, resist the urge to pull or chip ice off of loaded branches — you're almost always better off letting the ice melt on its own.

Why Regular Trimming Matters

It's tempting to skip maintenance on a tree that "looks fine." But the three reasons to trim regularly aren't cosmetic. Routine pruning keeps trees healthy by removing dead or diseased wood before it spreads, keeps them safe by thinning heavy limbs and correcting bad structure before something fails, and keeps them manageable so you never end up with a single enormous job after a decade of neglect. Done properly, a good pruning schedule extends the useful life of a tree by years, sometimes decades.

If you've got trees that haven't been looked at in a while, a professional walk-through is an easy place to start. It takes about twenty minutes for an experienced eye to tell you what's healthy, what's urgent, and what can wait.
